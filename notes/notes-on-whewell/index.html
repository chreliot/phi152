Notes on Whewell

Whewell

The way we can think about collections of observations is to invoke hypotheses.

Speculatively, imaginatively inventing hypotheses is necessary for scientific discovery (“invention”).

  • We only get true hypotheses by considering and rejecting many false ones.
  • History of science shows creative invention of many hypotheses is the rule rather than the exception.
    • Johannes Kepler only yielded his laws of motion (orbits are elliptical) through wild speculation, and Kepler’s work was necessary for Newton’s.

How does this yield a scientific method or logic?

The rigor of science comes from rigorously comparing hypotheses with facts and resolutely rejecting all which the comparison does not confirm.

The method looks like: wildly invent, scrupulously reject.

Induction is figuring out when a hypotheses closely matches the data.

A “consilience of inductions” is what we get when an Induction over one set of facts coincides with (matches, aligns with) one over another set of facts.

Example: gravity and the law of universal gravitation (Newton’s fourth law) explains and unifies:

  • falling objects
  • active movement
  • movement of planets
  • tides

Each of these classes of phenomena exhibit their own patterns. We identify those patterns inductively. And these inductions all hang together in a unified consilience. This consilience involves seeing familiar data in a new way, in terms of a new concept. That the consilience emerges is evidence for the correctness of the component inductions.

This justifies some inductive reasoning, and offers a method:

  1. Wildly hypothesize!
  2. Scrupulously reject hypotheses by carefully comparing with facts.
  3. See which hypotheses can be fit together in a consilience.
  4. Treat this unification as justification of the component inductions.
