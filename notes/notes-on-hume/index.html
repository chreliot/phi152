Notes on Hume

Hume

Knowledge

  • relations of ideas: the way ideas are connected [“3 x 5 = 30 / 2”, “Everything accurate is correct."] - true as a function of the meanings of the ideas involved.
    • A statement for which, once we define the ideas, it’s necessarily true.
  • matters of fact: [“Moisture can cause mold.” “Turtles have shells."]
    • A statement for which its contrary is still possible, in that it doesn’t imply a contradiction, and it can be conceived clearly by the mind. [contrary: “Moisture doesn’t cause mold."]

(contrary of “3 x 5 = 15” is “3 x 5 ≠ 15” equivalent to “1 ≠ 1” — that can’t be true.)

Hume applies this distinction to: “The sun will rise tomorrow.” It is a matter of fact.

How are the various observations of the sun rising connected to this further claim? The past risings must be connected to tomorrow’s sunrise, if we can infer anything about tomorrow’s from the past. How do we come to know about that?

We can come to know about anything through two kinds of reasoning:

  • deductive (“demonstrative”)
  • inductive (“probabilistic”)

Hume will argue that neither of these kinds of reasoning can support that the sun will rise tomorrow.

Reconstruction

We’re discussing reasoning about claims like “All A’s are B’s” The next A that we observe will be B.

(This is a case of enumerative induction.)

Enumerative induction: Inferring about a new case based on adding up past cases.

Can we justify the use of enumerative induction? It would need to be through deductive or inductive reasoning. It would require support for

PUN (“Principle of the Uniformity of Nature”): The future will be like the past.

This is a hidden premise in the argument “All A’s have been B’s, So, the next A will also be B.” (the inductive argument) principle is assumed in any support for matters of fact like that the sun will rise tomorrow.

Can we justify the PUN? If we can justify it, the reasoning would proceed

  • deductively? (But it is not entailed by anything in our observations. It can’t be deduced. Nothing in our experience guarantees the PUN is true.)
  • inductively? e.g., PUN has been true in the past, so it will be true in the future. (circular!: Any inductive argument for the PUN must rely on the PUN itself. This is “begging the question,” assuming what you’re trying to prove.)

So, there is no way of reasoning in support of conclusions like “The sun will rise tomorrow.” So, there’s no logical reason Billy’s inductive principle must be wrong.

Hume’s solution

When we expect outcomes, inductively, that’s our psychology working, not our rationality. Our psychology has us project the past into the future. But this isn’t logical.
