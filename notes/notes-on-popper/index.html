Notes on Popper

Popper’s criteria for falsifiability

  • A theory makes predictions, which are
    • capable of being false
    • precise (not vague)
    • about observable phenomena
    • risky, unexpected or improbable based on current theories
  • The theory must actually be open to rejection if its predictions are false (if it’s “falsified”), not preserved by ad hoc excuses, exceptions, or reinterpretations
  • not structurally resistant to falsification
  • not treated as resistant to falsification

“ad hoc” = “for this” = introduced for a specific purpose

So, when should we believe a theory?

Confirming evidence should count only when it results from a genuine effort to reject the theory — a serious but unsuccessful attempt to falsify it.

A theory is “corroborated” when it is tested seriously as much as possible, and it survives those falsification efforts. (Each of them corroborates it.)

Criticisms of Popper’s theory

  • Theoretical and modeling work often isn’t open to falsification, but we count it as scientific
  • Hilary Putnam: As yet unfalsified conjectures are not what we want from science — we want truths, to depend on for technological purposes. But: Corroboration, not “truth”, is the best science can do.
  • Imre Lakatos: Scientists reject many potential falsifications as not falsifying the theory ; they save it ad hoc. And that’s rational! But: a more sophisticated account of falsification than the sketch above might help.
