Topic 7: Randomized Controlled Trials

Reading

  1. “Randomized Experimental Design,” section 8.2 of Giere et al. Understanding Scientific Reasoning
  2. “Understanding and Misunderstanding Randomized Controlled Trials” by Angus Deaton and Nancy Cartwright. Social Science & Medicine 210:2–21 (August 2018). Read the abstract, sections 2.2, 3.2, 3.7, 3.8, and 4.
  3. “Prospective Designs” through “Retrospective Designs,” sections 8.5–8.7 of Giere et al. Understanding Scientific Reasoning

See also

  • “Randomized Controlled Trials and Evidence-Based Medicine,” section 5 of “Philosophy of Medicine,” by Julian Reiss and Rachel Ankeny in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

Learning questions

  1. What is the standard way of studying causation in populations? How does it work?
  2. What reason do we have, based on the structure of this research method, for thinking that it can sometimes reveal evidence of causal relationships?
  3. What are the main arguments Deaton and Cartwright offer about problems with randomized controlled trials?
  4. Why might randomized controlled trials not be possible for some investigations of possible causal relationships?
  5. What are two alternative approaches to investigating causation in populations when randomized controlled trials are not possible? What are each of their limitations?
