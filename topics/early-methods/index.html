Topic 2: Early Methods

As modern science emerged, its practitioners tried to describe how they were reasoning, and how others should reason, to do science. They came to quite different answers! This unit looks quickly at a few landmarks from the period 1600–1900.

Reading

  1. Francis Bacon, The New Organon (1620). Read Book 2, sections 10–16, starting on page 53 of the linked PDF. Focus on the figuring out the meaning of the beginning and end of the reading, and skim through the content in the middle to get a general sense of what’s going. (See reading questions below.)
  2. Isaac Newton, Regulae Philosophandi (Rules of Philosophy) as published in Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica (1687, 1726), 2 pages total
  3. David Hume, Part 1 of “Section 4: Sceptical doubts about the operations of the understanding,” from Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding (1748). That’s pages 11–14 of this PDF.
    • You can also (optionally) find an audio version of it on this page.
    • The assigned Early Modern Texts version has been modernized from the original, in word choice and sentence structure. You may check out the original, 18th century English version as Hume wrote it here.
  4. Wiliam Whewell, excerpts from Philosophy of the Inductive Sciences (1840, 1858), available here, about 4 pages total.
  5. Carl Hempel, “Scientific Inquiry: Invention and Test”, Chapter 2 of Philosophy of Natural Science (1966); note that the link includes more than just Chapter 2; your reading is 15 pages total.

Learning questions

  1. In New Organon, Bacon develops a new scientific method. We are going to figure out how it works. In the section I assigned you, he describes how to use it to answer the question What is heat? So: How does Bacon investigate what heat is?

Consider

  1. Is Bacon’s method a good way of learning what heat is?
  2. Is Bacon’s method “the scientific method”? Why or why not?
