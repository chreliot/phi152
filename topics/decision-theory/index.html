Topic 7: Decision Theory

Decision theory offers a framework for using knowledge to make decisions. To do this, it offers a way of integrating probabilities and values. And it offers some guidelines for choosing different probability/value combinations under different conditions.

Reading

  1. Hacking, “Expected Value,” chapter 8 of Introduction to Probability and Inductive Logic. Read pages 79–82 with care.
  2. Hacking, “Maximizing Expected Value,” chapter 9 of Introduction to Probability and Inductive Logic. Read pages 98–108.
  3. Hacking, “Decision under Uncertainty,” chapter 10 of Introduction to Probability and Inductive Logic

Optional reading

  • I’ve selected 15 total pages for you from Chapters 8–9, but there is a lot else that’s interesting in those chapters. In particular, they explore some of the paradoxes that arise from the kind of thinking introduced in the earlier parts of the chapters. It’s a classic philosophy: here’s the part that’s clear, and then here’s where the same ideas turn out to have strange, even worrisome implications. So, you may be interested, especially if the early parts of the chapters feel straightforward for you. But they are not required.

Learning questions

  1. What is an expected value, what does it apply to, and how can one calculate it?
  2. In what kinds of cases might we want to maximize expected value? In what kinds of cases might we not want to?
  3. What are the three main components of a decision problem?
  4. What’s the dominance rule?
  5. What’s the dominant expected value rule?
  6. What are satisfaction and security levels?

Consider

  1. What are the different things “utility” or “value” can refer to? Is it a vague concept?
  2. Where can the probabilities in a decision problem come from? Are they straightforward facts, or what?
  3. What is involved in using decision theory to decide public policy?
