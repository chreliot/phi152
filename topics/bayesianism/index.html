Topic 4: Bayesianism

Bayesianism, as an approach to inductive reasoning, focuses on updating our beliefs in light of new evidence. It is built around a simple formula that expresses a relationship between probabilities.

Reading

  1. Hacking, Chapter 7 “Bayes’ Rule” (pages 69–77)
  2. Hacking, Chapter 15 “Learning from Experience” (pages 171–188)
  3. Hacking, Chapter 21 “Learning from Experience as an Evasion of the Problem of Induction” (4 pages) — and if you’re not feeling sufficiently familiar with “the problem of induction” (from our discussion of Hume), optionally look at the first pages of Chapter 20

Learning questions

  1. What do each of the terms mean in the basic version of Bayes’s Rule?
  2. What does Bayes’s Rule do best? What is its main function in scientific reasoning?
  3. Why are likelihood terms an important part of Bayes’s Rule?
  4. What are some limitations (or criticisms) of using Bayes’s Rule as a universal strategy for justifying scientific knowledge?
    • How can Bayesian philosophers respond to those criticisms?
