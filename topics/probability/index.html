Topic 3: Probability

Here we set up some basic ideas about probability which will are necessary for understanding the approaches to scientific justification scientists have used most during the last fifty years.

Reading

  1. Hacking, Introduction to Probability and Inductive Logic
    • Chapter 3 “The Gambler’s Fallacy”
      • This chapter introduces the basic concepts randomness, independence, and probability model but overall it’s pitched to give you a feel for some probability ideas. You might read it more quickly than Chapter 4↓.
    • Chapter 4 “Elementary Probability Ideas”
      • As noted above, this is a more foundational chapter.
    • Chapter 5 “Conditional Probability”
    • Chapter 11 “What Do You Mean?”
    • Chapter 12 “Theories of Probability”

Learning questions

  1. Give examples of situations that succeed at being, in turn: random, independent, good probability models.
  2. Give examples of situations that fail at being, in turn: random, independent, good probability models.

See also

  1. Hacking, Chapter 6 “The Basic Rules of Probability” is not assigned but summarizes the rules that appear in the above assigned chapters.
  2. Alan Hájek’s “Interpretations of Probability” in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy is a much deeper account of this topic, but especially Hacking Chapter 11.
