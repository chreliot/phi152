Topic 1: Logic

Does science have a logic? Could it be what we call “deductive logic,” (which is the focus of other courses like PHI 154 Symbolic Logic)? What is deductive logic? Is non-deductive logic possible? What could “inductive logic” be? Could it be the logic of science?

Reading

(This is what you should read for this section. The due dates appear on the front page schedule.)

  1. Salmon, Wesley C. “The Scope of Logic,” chapter 1 of Logic, 1984. We’ll read parts 1 and 2 of this chapter (“Argument” and “Inference”) for our first discussion and then parts 3 and 4 (“Discovery and Justification” and “Deductive and Inductive Arguments”) for the second.

Handout

  1. Ways of thinking about validity

Learning questions

(These questions reflect some of the main things—not necessarily all the things—I suggest you learn for this section.)

  1. What does deductive logic describe?
  2. What makes an argument deductively valid? … invalid? … sound? … unsound?
  3. What are some examples of deductively valid arguments?
  4. How can an if-then statement express necessary and sufficient conditions?
  5. What is the difference between deductive and inductive reasoning?
  6. What’s an example of a completely deductive scientific method?
  7. Why would it be a good thing if we could reason scientifically using only deductive logic (and not inductive reasoning)?

Consider

(These more open-ended questions reflect some things I hope this material will have you thinking about for yourself.)

  1. Could a computer reason scientifically? Could one do science?
  2. What’s the relationship between deductive logic and good reasoning more generally?
