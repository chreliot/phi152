Topic 5: Frequentism

Next, we examine the second main, contemporary approach to scientific reasoning, based in the frequentist understanding of probability. At the heart of its reasoning is the “double negative” inference, based in the “probabilistic modus tollens.” The jargon name for this approach is “null hypothesis significance testing.” We will observe how and why it works, building up from some foundational concepts.

Reading

  1. Hacking, Chapter 16 “Stability”
  2. Hacking, Chapter 17 “Normal Approximations”
  3. Hacking, Chapter 18 “Significance and Power” (note on type I/II error)

Slides

  1. Introducing null-hypothesis testing
  2. Introduction to population sampling and correlation

Learning questions

  1. How does the relative frequency interpretation of probability give rise to a way of justifying scientific knowledge?
  2. What is the “double negative” question at the heart of the frequentist approach we’ve called “null hypothesis significance testing”?
  3. What’s a correlation? Illustrate an example with a box diagram.
  4. How can we use sampling theory to tell whether a correlation is significant?
  5. Where do margins of error come from?
  6. What does a p value measure?
  7. If we test a hypothesis using null hypothesis significance testing and we get a statistically significant result, does that tell us that the hypothesis is true, or not, or what?
  8. What are some criticisms of using frequentist methods to justify scientific knowledge?
